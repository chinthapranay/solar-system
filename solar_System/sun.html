The Sun

Our Solar System's Radiant Heart

Key Facts About Our Sun

Basic Characteristics

Classification

G-type main-sequence star (G2V)

Age

Approximately 4.6 billion years

Mass

1.989 × 10³⁰ kg (333,000 Earths)

Diameter

1.3927 million km (109 × Earth)

Physical Properties

Surface Temperature

5,500°C (9,932°F)

Core Temperature

15 million °C (27 million °F)

Rotation Period

25-35 days (varies by latitude)

Axial Tilt

7.25° relative to ecliptic

Solar Composition

Hydrogen

Approximately 74% of the Sun's mass is hydrogen, which serves as the primary fuel for nuclear fusion.

Helium

Helium makes up about 24% of the Sun's mass, a byproduct of the fusion process.

Heavy Elements

The remaining 2% consists of heavier elements like oxygen, carbon, neon, and iron.

Formation Timeline

Solar Nebula

4.6 Billion Years Ago - A giant molecular cloud of gas and dust began collapsing under its own gravity.

Protostar Phase

As the cloud collapsed, it formed a spinning disk with a hot, dense protostar at the center.

T Tauri Phase

The young Sun cleared its neighborhood of gas and dust through intense solar winds.

Main Sequence

Nuclear fusion of hydrogen to helium began, marking the Sun's entry into its current stable phase.

Planetary Nebula

In about 5 billion years, the Sun will expand into a red giant and shed its outer layers, creating a beautiful planetary nebula.

White Dwarf

After the planetary nebula phase, the Sun's core will collapse into a dense white dwarf, slowly cooling over billions of years.

Stellar Evolution

Life Cycle of Stars

Stars evolve through different stages based on their mass. Our Sun, being a medium-sized star, will eventually become a red giant, then a planetary nebula, and finally a white dwarf. More massive stars can end their lives as neutron stars or black holes.

Solar Structure

Core

The Sun's core is the central region where nuclear fusion occurs, converting hydrogen into helium. It's incredibly dense (150 g/cm³) and hot (15 million °C), generating 99% of the Sun's energy through the proton-proton chain reaction.

Radiative Zone

Extending from the core to about 70% of the Sun's radius, this region transfers energy through radiation. Photons can take up to 170,000 years to travel through this dense region, constantly being absorbed and re-emitted.

Convective Zone

The outer 30% of the Sun's radius where hot plasma rises in convection currents, carrying energy to the surface. This creates the Sun's granulation pattern and contributes to the solar dynamo that generates the Sun's magnetic field.

Atmosphere

The Sun's atmosphere has three main layers:

  • Photosphere: The visible surface layer, about 500 km thick, with a temperature of about 5,500°C. This is where sunspots and faculae appear.
  • Chromosphere: A reddish layer about 2,000 km thick, visible during total solar eclipses. Temperature increases with height from 4,000°C to 25,000°C.
  • Corona: The outermost layer, extending millions of kilometers into space. Despite being less dense, it's much hotter (1-3 million °C) than the surface - a phenomenon known as the coronal heating problem.
